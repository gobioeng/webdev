Exciting Careers in Radiotherapy and Biomedical Engineering

Shaping the Future of Healthcare in the UK with the NHS

Tanmay Pandey
Editor, GoBioEng
Updated: August 23, 2025
10 min read

Looking for a career that combines technology and helping people? Biomedical engineering with the NHS could be perfect for you. It's not just a job – it's a chance to make a real difference in patients' lives while working with cutting-edge technology.

Quick Facts: NHS Engineering Careers

  • The NHS employs over 5,000 healthcare scientists and engineers across the UK
  • Biomedical engineers typically start at Band 5 (£27,055 - £32,934) and can progress to Band 8 (£56,164 - £91,787)
  • The NHS has more than 400 different career paths
  • 15% expected growth in healthcare engineering jobs over the next decade

Source: NHS Careers, NHS Digital, NHS Employers

Why Biomedical Engineering?

Biomedical engineering sits where engineering meets healthcare. You'll work with life-saving equipment, develop new medical technologies, and help doctors provide better care to patients. This field is perfect if you:

  • Enjoy solving technical problems
  • Want to help people in a hands-on way
  • Like working with advanced technology
  • Are interested in healthcare and medicine

As a biomedical engineer, you could design artificial limbs, maintain MRI machines, test new medical devices, or ensure hospital equipment works correctly. Your work directly affects patient care - when you keep machines running smoothly, you help save lives.

An NHS biomedical engineer working on radiotherapy equipment

Real-World Impact: 3D-Printed Prosthetics

One exciting area is 3D printing for healthcare. The NHS has partnered with companies like Open Bionics to create affordable, custom-made prosthetic limbs for patients.

For example, at the Royal Liverpool University Hospital, biomedical engineers used 3D printing to create a prosthetic hand for a young patient at about one-tenth the cost of traditional prosthetics. The hand was not only functional but designed to look like a superhero hand, which helped the child feel more confident and positive about their prosthetic.

The NHS: Your Pathway to Success

The National Health Service (NHS) is the UK's largest employer, with over 1.3 million staff. It offers excellent opportunities for biomedical engineers at all career stages.

Working for the NHS has many benefits:

  • Job security - healthcare always needs skilled engineers
  • Clear career path with opportunities to advance
  • Competitive pay with regular increases as you progress
  • Excellent pension scheme that employers contribute to
  • Work-life balance with flexible working options
  • 27+ days annual leave plus bank holidays
  • Training and development opportunities
  • Chance to work on cutting-edge technology
  • The satisfaction of helping patients every day

NHS Statistics: A Growing Field

According to the NHS Long Term Plan, the need for healthcare engineers is expected to grow by 15% over the next decade. This growth is happening because:

  • The UK has an aging population that needs more healthcare
  • Hospitals are using more advanced technology
  • There's increased focus on early diagnosis and precision treatments
  • The NHS is expanding its digital and AI capabilities

Apprenticeships: Learn While You Earn

Worried about university costs? NHS apprenticeships let you earn a salary while getting your qualifications. You'll spend part of your time studying and part working alongside experienced professionals. It's a great way to:

  • Avoid student debt
  • Get practical, hands-on experience
  • Earn while you learn
  • Build professional connections

The NHS offers several apprenticeship paths:

  • Healthcare Science Associate Apprenticeship (Level 4) - Entry with GCSEs in math, English, and science
  • Healthcare Science Practitioner Apprenticeship (Level 6) - Equivalent to a bachelor's degree
  • Healthcare Science Degree Apprenticeship - Full BSc (Hons) qualification

For example, the Northern Care Alliance NHS Foundation Trust offers a 4-year Healthcare Science (Medical Engineering) Apprenticeship where you work and study simultaneously. You'll spend one day per week at university and four days in the hospital, earning a full salary starting at over £20,000 per year.

"My NHS apprenticeship was the best decision I ever made. I'm earning money, getting a degree, and already have three years of real work experience. The best part is seeing how the equipment I help maintain directly impacts patient care."

— James Taylor, Healthcare Science Apprentice, University Hospitals Birmingham NHS Foundation Trust

Radiotherapy Engineering: A Specialized Path

One exciting specialty is radiotherapy engineering. This field focuses on the technology used to treat cancer patients with radiation.

As a radiotherapy engineer, you'll work with amazing technology like:

  • Linear accelerators (LINACs) - Advanced machines that deliver precise radiation doses to tumors while minimizing damage to healthy tissue
  • CT simulators - Specialized CT scanners used to plan treatments
  • Treatment planning systems - Complex software that determines exactly how to target radiation
  • Proton beam therapy systems - Cutting-edge treatment that uses protons instead of X-rays

Spotlight: Proton Beam Therapy

The NHS now offers proton beam therapy at two specialized centers:

  • The Christie NHS Foundation Trust in Manchester (opened 2018)
  • University College London Hospital (opened 2021)

These £125 million facilities use massive particle accelerators to target tumors with unprecedented precision. Radiotherapy engineers working here maintain some of the most advanced medical equipment in the world.

Before these centers opened, NHS patients needing proton therapy had to travel abroad for treatment, often to the USA. Now, over 1,500 patients per year can receive this advanced treatment within the UK.

Radiotherapy engineers make sure these highly complex machines work perfectly. Even a tiny error could affect patient treatment, so accuracy and attention to detail are essential. It's a role where your technical skills directly help save lives.

Real-World Impact: MR-Linac Technology

One of the most exciting recent developments in radiotherapy is the MR-Linac, which combines an MRI scanner with a linear accelerator. This allows doctors to see real-time images of tumors during treatment, making radiation therapy even more precise.

The NHS has been at the forefront of implementing this technology, with the first UK MR-Linac installed at The Royal Marsden in London. Engineers working on these systems need knowledge of both MRI and radiotherapy technology, making this a challenging but highly rewarding specialty.

A Bright Future Awaits

A biomedical engineering career with the NHS offers continuous growth. You might start as a junior engineer and progress to leading a department, specializing in research, or moving into management.

The NHS career framework makes it clear how to advance:

  1. Start as a Junior/Assistant Engineer (Band 5)
  2. Progress to Biomedical Engineer (Band 6)
  3. Become a Senior Engineer with supervisory duties (Band 7)
  4. Advance to Principal Engineer or specialist role (Band 8a)
  5. Reach Department Head/Regional Lead (Bands 8b-8d)

You can also move between different specialties, such as:

  • Medical equipment management
  • Radiotherapy engineering
  • Rehabilitation engineering
  • Clinical measurements
  • Renal technology
  • Equipment evaluation

Emerging Trends: AI in Healthcare

Artificial intelligence is changing healthcare, and the NHS is investing heavily in this technology. AI is now being used to:

  • Analyze medical scans to detect cancer earlier
  • Predict which patients might develop complications
  • Help plan radiation treatments more precisely
  • Monitor equipment performance to prevent breakdowns

Engineers with skills in both healthcare technology and AI/data science will be particularly valuable in the coming years. The NHS AI Lab, launched with £250 million funding, is creating many new opportunities in this field.

How to Get Started

Ready to launch your NHS biomedical engineering career? Here are your options:

1. University Route

Get a degree in biomedical engineering, electrical/electronic engineering, mechanical engineering, physics, or a related field. Then apply to the NHS Scientific Training Programme (STP) or directly to engineering positions.

2. Apprenticeship Route

Apply for an NHS apprenticeship in Healthcare Science (Medical Engineering). You'll need good GCSEs in math, English, and science. This route lets you earn while you learn, with no tuition fees.

3. Transfer from Another Engineering Field

If you already have engineering experience, you might be able to transfer into healthcare engineering through NHS positions that provide on-the-job training.
